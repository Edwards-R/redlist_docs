# Assessment-Model summary
This page documents the various methods by with the Red List assessments have been applied to the available data. Where a taxon has been used, the criteria for meeting `Near Threatened` has also been documented.

To ensure the best utilisation of the imperfect data available, this Red List has examined the data using multiple models. Each model is designed, where permitted by the Red List specification, to act with a different set of assumptions and priorities. By overlapping these models, a much clearer - although still very blurry - picture of reality may be obtained.

## Criterion A
### A1
The current state of research into this taxon group is insufficient to be able to utilise A1. We do not fully understand the causes of decline at an appropriate level.

### A2
Taxa have been assessed against A2 using a range of models.

#### Subcriteria
Of the subcriteria, (b) & (c) are the only applicable subcriteria. The dataset does not meet the requirements for (a) as the level of 'direct observation' is not reached. (d) is inapplicable as the taxa in question are not farmed or hunted (other than *Apis mellifera*, which is considered to be almost entirely domesticated in the region). (e) has potential for future assessments, but the current state of research into the taxa is insufficient for us to use this in this assessment. In particular, it may be of interest to examine future potential in regards to competitors. However, it order to obtain a suitable answer there will need to be a number of taxon-specific research projects.

#### Near Threatened
Statistical models are regarded as being too imprecise to support an additional threshold between 0 and -30% change. Year-on-year variation in survey effort, as well as weather effect, make counts per year extremely vulnerable to swings and changes.

NT has been permitted for use under the 'Expert Inference' model. The criteria for inclusion as NT under A2 (Expert Inference) is either:

- The taxon has notably declined over the entire assessed region
- The decline is less than a 30% uniform decline

**or**

- The taxon has notably declined with a significant sub-region of the assessed region
- The decline within the significant subregion is greater than 30%

### A3
No model is able to support direct use of A3 for any taxa. The primary long-term threat for the assessed group is climate related. However, climate does not operate on the 10-year resolution that the Red List requests.

#### Near Threatened
A3 has been used to assess long-term, fuzzy, impacts of climate change. The time horizon for these impacts is set at 100 years. The overall scenario assumes:

- An overall rise in average summer temperature allowing other taxa to colonise from the European continent
- An overall rise in extreme events, particularly storm surges

It is felt that this scenario is far too imprecise and uncertain to be able to utilise  the full range of Criterion A. Accordingly, this long time horizon has been restricted to a maximum threat status of NT under the basis of a *suspected* change - the lowest data quality available under the Red List criteria. The assessment is performed by taxon experts rather than statistical models, as there do not exist statistical models to perform this task.

## A4
This criterion does not have any available statistical models to assess. There is also a significant lack of knowledge around causes of decline, especially projected into the future with any reliable accuracy (see A3). As a result, this criterion is not used for this assessment.

## Criterion B
### B1
This criterion has been used as described in the Red List **Guidance** documentation.

### B2
This criterion has been used as described in the Red List **Guidance** documentation.

### Subcriteria (B-generic)
Subcriteria under B do not differ between B1 and B2. As such, the subcritera for these two assessments may be treated once.
#### (a)
Few taxa surveyed in enough intensity to be able to use 'Severely Fragmented'. As part of preliminary examination of the data, most observed severe fragmentation was deemed to be the result of poor survey quality rather than verified 'true' fragmentation.

#### (b)
Of the sub-subcriteria, (i), (ii), (iii), (iv), and (v) were all applied. Notably, (i) was measured using discrete extent of occurrence (buffer-union). (iii), (iv), and (v) do not have statistical models to analyse, and so were assigned on the basis of consultation with general taxon experts.

### Near Threatened (B-generic)
Subcriteria under B do not differ between B1 and B2. Given that NT for B in this assessment relies solely upon a variation in the thresholds of the subcriteria, both B1 and B2 share the same subcriteria assessment for reaching NT.

#### Near Threatened
A taxon is considered NT if:

- The headline decline meets the `VU` threshold (< 20,000 km² for B1, < 2,000 km² for B2)
- There is a >= 30% decline, between time slices 1 & 3, in either
    - discrete extent of occupancy (measured by buffer-union)
    - area of occupancy

## C
No assessed taxa have data population count level data nor, to any of the consulted experts, is such data obtainable at any reliable level. As such, **C** has not been used.

## D
>Assessment 'D' under **D** will be referred to as 'D1' to avoid confusion.

### D1
This assessment requires counts of individuals. As per **C**, no such counts are available and this assessment has not been used.

### D2
This assessment has been used, with caution. An area of occupancy below 20 km² typically denotes poor survey effort/detectability. Only taxa which are *strongly* suspected to have a true area of occupancy below the threshold are considered for D2.

#### Near Threatened
Given the difficulty in obtaining true area of occupancy at very small scales, there is deemed to be no supportable means of achieving `NT` under D2 without resulting in a repeat of **B**. As such, `NT` is not achievable under D2

## E
No model exists which can measure the probability of extinction in the wild, and no data exists to reliably generate such a model. Weather, as the short-term measure of climate, is too chaotic and the linkage between weather and individual taxa is too poorly understood. **E** is therefore not used.
